A RIVER OF ICE ABOVE CHAMONIX

Mer de Glace

The largest glacier in the French Alps. Take the red Montenvers train from Chamonix, then hike up and into the white.

From Chamonix, catch the Montenvers train to its upper station. The view opens up the whole glacier. From there, 45 minutes to an hour of hiking gets you onto the better vantage points along the moraine.

Anytime light works. Sunset, sunrise, moody overcast, or a clear night for stars. Bring warm layers; the wind coming off the ice strips the heat fast even in summer.

RegionChamonix, France
AccessTrain + 45-60min hike
EffortModerate
Best lightAnytime (sunset / sunrise best)

THE SAME GLACIER, UNDER SNOW

Mer de Glace. Winter

Same spot, deep winter. Snowshoes on, footprints few, the whole scene turned silent and white.

Exact same access as the summer version. Montenvers train, hike up. But bring snowshoes. The trail gets covered fast and you'll sink without them.

The icefall textures read differently under fresh snow. Everything looks quieter, cleaner, a bit more alien. Go on a clear morning after a storm if you can time it.

RegionChamonix, France
AccessTrain + snowshoe hike
EffortModerate. Winter gear
Best lightMorning after fresh snow

TWIN SPIRES OVER THE GLACIER

Les Drus

Two near-vertical granite spires above the Mer de Glace. From the Montenvers viewpoint, they line up clean against the sky.

Seen from the same Mer de Glace vantages you hiked for the glacier shots. No separate trip needed. Shoot them on the same outing with a longer lens to isolate.

Sunset and sunrise both hit the granite at a nice angle. Moody overcast gives the rock real character. Blue-sky blue-hour is the weakest window here.

RegionChamonix, France
AccessFrom Mer de Glace viewpoint
EffortModerate
Best lightSunset, sunrise, or moody

THE SPIRES IN WINTER

Les Drus. Winter

Same twin spires, dressed in ice and snow. The contrast against dark granite is what makes the winter shot sing.

Same access as summer version. Montenvers train, then snowshoes along the ridge vantages above the glacier. Stop and recompose every few minutes as the angles shift.

Clear conditions help the rock/snow contrast read; heavy cloud softens it too much. A long lens (200mm+) compresses the scene the way you want it.

Another frame of Les Drus from the Mer de Glace viewpoint. Same mountain, different compression, different mood.

From the same Mer de Glace vantages, swap to a longer focal length and recompose. The ridge lines stack differently when you compress them at 300mm vs. 100mm.

This one was shot at sunset with warm light grazing the spires. Sunrise works too if the wind is calm; moody overcast gives a dramatic grey-scale rendition.

A POINTED NEEDLE IN THE RANGE

Aiguille du Grépon

A sharp granite needle near the Mer de Glace. A classic alpine silhouette when the light catches the ridge.

Visible from the Mer de Glace approach. No separate trek. You'll pass the right angle on the same walk that gets you the glacier shots.

Sunrise and sunset both work. Moody weather with torn cloud gives you the most dramatic rendition. The needle in and out of the mist looks straight out of an old alpinism print.

THE BEST VIEW OF MONT BLANC

Les Chéserys

A cluster of small lakes above La Flégère with the cleanest view of Mont Blanc and the whole surrounding range.

Park in La Flégère, take the cable car up, then hike to the lakes. Lac Blanc is part of the same system and worth the extra walk. Bigger lake, equally good reflections.

Sunset is the hit. Mont Blanc and the range behind the lakes light up orange, and if the wind drops, you get the whole thing mirrored in the water. Plan your descent with a head torch if you stay for blue hour.

RegionChamonix, France
AccessCable car + hike
EffortModerate

HIKE AROUND THE LAKES TO FIND YOUR FRAME

Mighty Range

Same lakes area, different time of day. Daylight walks around Les Chéserys are how you find the compositions for the golden-hour shots.

Walk around the lake system during the day to scout compositions. Find the rocks, the reflections, the foreground frames that will work when the light turns. Then come back at sunset or sunrise fully prepared.

Both sunset and sunrise work here. Sunrise puts first light on the range; sunset is warmer but with haze risk from a full day's heating. Morning is usually the cleaner shot.

RegionChamonix, France
AccessSame as Les Chéserys
EffortEasy. Scouting
Best lightSunset or sunrise

BIVY UP HIGH, ABOVE THE CROWDS

Camping in Chéserys

Wild camping at Les Chéserys is allowed. You just need to register online first. Find a quiet spot off the main drag.

Camping is permitted but regulated. Register online before you go; you'll fill out a short questionnaire. Details are posted at the La Flégère cable car station.

The area around the main lakes gets busy. Walk a bit further, find an empty spot with your own view. We've camped a ridge away from the main lake and had the whole scene to ourselves.

PROTECTED TERRAIN, WILDLIFE EVERYWHERE

Aiguille Rouge Reserve

The Chéserys area sits inside a protected nature reserve. Ibex, chamois, marmot. Real chances if you stay quiet and patient.

Never chase wildlife. A 200mm+ lens is what you want; step back and let them approach your line of sight. Mornings are the most active window. Dawn to maybe 10 a.m.

The reserve status means strict rules around camping, drones, and leaving no trace. Read the signs at the cable car station and stick to the rules. It's the reason the place still works.

AccessSame as Les Chéserys
EffortEasy

WESTERN EUROPE'S HIGHEST. WITH A TELE

Mont Blanc

From Les Chéserys, Mont Blanc sits enormous across the valley. A telephoto lens turns the view into detail-rich alpine portraiture.

Bring a 100-400mm. The range reveals ridges, couloirs, and texture you can't see with a wide lens. This is the spot where reach pays off. Compress the scene and pick out the features.

Sunset and blue hour are the windows. Blue hour lasts longer at altitude and gives you that soft, saturated palette that makes the mountain look almost unreal. This one was shot at sunset on a 100-400.

RegionChamonix, France
AccessFrom Les Chéserys
EffortEasy. Standing still
Best lightSunset or blue hour

MY FAVOURITE GLACIER OF THE RANGE

Glacier des Bossons

The Bossons drops farther into the Chamonix valley than any other glacier in the range. Which means you can see it from almost everywhere in town.

Visible from the Chamonix valley floor. Many angles work; pick a foreground. A chalet, a treeline, a bend of the river. And anchor the glacier in it.

Anytime light is fine. Late afternoon warms the ice and catches the highlights. A good viewpoint is from above, on the Aiguille du Midi cable car route. Or with a drone if you're somewhere you can fly legally.

RegionChamonix, France
AccessFrom Chamonix valley
EffortEasy
Best lightAny time

PEAKS

Pic de Jallouvre

An off-radar Aravis summit above a tiny crescent lake. France, not Switzerland, but one ride away.

Start from the Col de la Colombière and take the ridge trail south toward Pointe Blanche, then push on to Pic de Jallouvre. Five hours round trip.

The crescent lake below the summit is the frame-maker. Sit on the rocks, put the lake in the corner, the Aravis chain filling the back.

Golden hour here is ridiculous. The whole range catches orange light while the valley fills with haze. Do not leave until the sun is gone.

RegionAravis, Haute-Savoie, France
AccessCol de la Colombière + ridge
EffortLong, 5h round
Best lightSunset

THE PEAK YOU SEE FROM LAVAUX

Dent d'Oche

Across the lake from Lavaux, on the French side. A long hike to a steep summit. The view back across Léman is everything.

The hike starts at the restaurant called Fétiuière. It's long and gets very steep toward the top. Bring poles, food, and time. Round trip is most of a day.

From the summit, Lac Léman spreads out below and the Alps roll north toward Switzerland. Sunset, sunrise, and moody conditions all deliver; pick a day with a stable weather window and you'll get one of the best Léman views of your life.

AccessDrive + long hike
EffortHard
Best lightSunset, sunrise, or moody
